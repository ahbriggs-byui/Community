Sleep Cycles

When you sleep, you cycle through two phases of sleep: rapid eye movement (REM) and non-REM sleep. The cycle starts over every 80 to 100 minutes. Usually there are four to six cycles per night.1

  • N-REM Stage 1: This is the lightest stage of sleep and typically occurs right after falling asleep. This stage is only present for 1-5 minutes and has the least amount of time spent in it. 2
  • N-REM Stage 2: This is the stage of sleep you spend the most amount of time in. This is where the brain consolidates memory and processes all the information that was learned through the course of the day and the actions that were done.
  • N-REM Stage 3: This is the deepest stage of sleep achieved during a night's rest. This stage is characterized by slow brain waves, and lowered arousal potential. During this stage the body repairs and regrows damaged tissues, bones and muscle. 2
  • REM: During REM the brain's activity mirrors the awake brain. This is when dreams and nightmares occur. 3

Adequate Sleep Times

Sleep is essential for people of all ages. The amount of sleep that people need varies from age to age and from person to person. As we age, our need for sleep decreases but never goes away. Sleep Disorders

There are a variety of sleep disorders present in our society. Listed down below are some of the common disorders that affect people on a daily basis. Sleeping disorders can affect people by disrupting the ability to sleep, impacting the effectiveness of sleep received, and impacting physical and mental health throughout the lifespan.

Etiology: The body’s internal clock does not match up to the external environmental clock causing the individual to have improper sleep times. Disturbances in light-dark cycle from shift work, jet lag, irregular sleep-wake rhythm disorders and other patterns that disrupt an individual's natural sleep cycle cause this in people of all ages.2

Treatment: The goal of treatment is to realign the body’s circadian rhythm with the desired schedule. This is done through light therapy, melatonin, medications, and/or chronotherapy.

Treatment: The treatments for insomnia include cognitive-behavioural therapy and medication

Narcolepsy can also be accompanied by cataplexy, which is a brief involuntary loss of muscle control following strong positive emotions. Narcolepsy is diagnosed through a doctor ordered sleep study.4

Treatment: Current treatments aim to treat symptoms. Medications to improve wakefulness during daytime hours and good sleep hygiene are the common modalities of treatment
